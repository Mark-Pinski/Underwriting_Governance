About Global Underwriting Governance

One home for the frameworks, tools and reporting that keep underwriting activity consistent, compliant and well documented — from the authority an underwriter is granted, to how that authority is reviewed and reported on.

What this site is for

Global Underwriting Governance brings together three things that used to live in separate, jurisdiction-specific places: Underwriting Authority management, Underwriting Quality (peer) Reviews, and Product Governance. The goal is one consistent framework, one place to find guidance, and one reporting layer that leadership can actually use to act on.

Who it's for

Underwriters — look up your own authority statement and applicable guidelines.

Submitters — request new, amended or expanded authority on behalf of your team.

Approvers — review and approve authority requests and peer review recommendations.

Global CUOs & Functional Leads — approve Tier 2 product initiatives and monitor governance KPIs.

How the pieces fit together

UW Tools & Guardrails defines what an underwriter is allowed to do.

Underwriting Quality Reviews checks that activity stays inside those guardrails.

Reporting & Analytics turns both into KPIs leadership can act on.

Live today

The frameworks that define what an underwriter can do, and how new or changed products get approved before they reach the market.

Underwriting Authority, in plain terms

An Underwriting Authority is best thought of like a driver's license: it names the product, jurisdiction and business segment you're permitted to write for ACME, and sets the maximum limits within which you can operate. Writing outside those limits — without referring the risk to someone who holds the right authority — is an authority violation.

Personal Underwriting Authority Statement

The first component of your authority. It conveys:

  • The product, jurisdiction and business segment you're permitted to write
  • Your capacity, i.e. maximum limits (Authority Level)
  • Any grants or restrictions for specific business (e.g., multinational risks)

Sample statement

You have been granted authority to write risks on a co-insurance basis up to ACME 50% share (max USD 25m any one risk), on accounts within the Business Package risk appetite and line sheet.

This authority is limited to Property risks located in New Zealand only.

Product Governance Framework

Applies to material product revisions, expansion into a new country, breakthrough/new products, and market entries or re-entries. Covers four elements: Forms, Rates, Rules, and Processes (including but not limited to Claims, Distribution, Operations and Reinsurance).

Tier 1

Global PD Governance

Material or significant revision of an existing product, or expansion into a new country. Applies when the change:

  • Changes ACME's risk profile (including regulator-driven changes)
  • Offers an existing product in a market where ACME is already present, for the first time
  • Reintroduces a product, or changes it, after it wasn't actively sold
  • Requires material changes to internal processes or resources
  • Uses a distribution channel or method new to that country

Tier 2

CUO + Functional Lead approval

Breakthrough / new products and market entries or re-entries. Applies when at least one condition is met:

  • Has not previously been offered by ACME in any country
  • Novel design or major overhaul that materially changes ACME's global risk profile
  • The first product offered in a new country for ACME
  • The first product for a market re-entry, 5+ years after exit

Underwriting Quality Reviews & Global Insurance UW Peer Review

Live today

Peer review exists because audit capacity is finite — most portfolios are examined by internal audit only once every few years. In between, senior underwriters in the field review the files their peers write, on a quarterly, semi-annual or annual cycle depending on the product.

Why peer review matters

A senior underwriter reviewing their own product line day to day catches things a periodic audit can miss — and catches them sooner. The value only holds up if reviews are actually completed, and if they surface real findings rather than becoming a checkbox exercise. A high completion percentage on its own doesn't tell leadership much; a queue with strong completion and zero recommendations for a year, sitting next to a short audit that finds several issues, is itself a signal worth a closer look.

Reading the numbers

A completion figure by itself — "117% of quota" — doesn't say whether that's a healthy program or a sign the quota itself needs revisiting. We pair completion with recommendation volume so a quiet queue and a busy one are easy to tell apart.

Where a product shows strong completion and no recommendations over an extended period, the follow-up question is usually about file selection: are the highest-risk files actually the ones being reviewed?

Data quality note

Service Programs' review population is small enough that individual cases stand out: this period's report flagged underwriters who were deprovisioned some time ago but still appear against an active quota. Where a reviewer left mid-period, they should appear for the period they were active and drop out after — we're reconciling a handful of these before the figures roll up to leadership.

Review cycle rules

Underwriters holding both a Warranty and a Plus designation require one reviewed account per year in each. Underwriters holding only a Plus designation require two reviewed accounts per year. Cross-regional reviews (e.g., an APAC reviewer covering a US portfolio) are supported but tracked separately by folder.

Why KPIs, not raw activity counts

Raw counts (files reviewed, letters issued) don't tell leadership whether the program is working. The KPIs planned for this dashboard pair completion against findings, so a quiet queue and a productive one are easy to tell apart at a glance — and current-period figures are always shown against the prior period, which is the comparison CUOs actually look for, not a five-year trend line.

Underwriter performance itself is judged the same way — by objective throughput and quality measures (files processed, policies written, loss ratio), not a manager's subjective read. A manager who doesn't want to lose a direct report has every incentive to rate them well regardless of performance, so the KPIs are what stands in for that.

What leadership sees today, and what changes

Product Development Governance reports on Personal Insurance products are shared with CUOs monthly. The near-term goal for this dashboard is to bring peer review and authority data to that same standard.

One thing worth building in deliberately: CEOs not reacting to a number isn't necessarily a good sign — it often just means they don't know what to do with it. A figure like "117% of quota" needs enough narrative attached that it prompts a real decision, not silence.
